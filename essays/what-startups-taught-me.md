- Don't think global: "Global" is what outsiders call America. In the Bay, people just talk about users, markets, distribution, and categories.
- Become native to the market: If you want American customers, don't "expand to the US"; live inside the market until your instincts become American.
- Korean users make you polished, SF users make you wrong faster: Korea rewards completeness; SF rewards being early, weird, broken, and directionally right.
- Traction is not signal: GitHub stars, Reddit launches, open-source love, and impressive inbound can all be real while still pointing you toward the wrong company.
- Not all users are your congregation: The founder, product, and users have to believe in the same religion.
- Moats are a late-stage anxiety: Early founders do not have anything to defend yet; the only early moat is taste, conviction, and proximity to users.
- The market does not care about your thesis: Local-first, open-source, privacy-first, and enterprise-safe can all be true and still not be enough.
- Moving fast can break trust: Shipping twenty-one versions was not the mistake; shipping without communicating was.
- A pivot means leaving some users behind: Sometimes the old congregation has to leave for the new product to exist.
- Don't build a better X: If the best description of your company is a better version of another company, you probably do not own the category.
- Distribution is product work: Distribution is not something you do after building; it changes what the product should be.
- Advice is median, responsibility is personal: YC advice, investor advice, and founder advice are useful, but the company belongs to the founder.
- Clear thinking beats startup momentum: Hiring, ads, features, and launches can all feel productive while avoiding the hard thing.
- Taste is a founder skill: Great products come from what repeatedly moves you, not just from market maps.
- A founder keeps becoming responsible: Leadership is starting things, taking responsibility, gathering strong people, and growing through them.
- Customer development is truth-seeking, not pitching: People lie about opinions and future behavior, but they tell the truth through past behavior and commitments.
- Ship it, then stay: Finished software is dead software; the real commitment is continuing to care after reality breaks the thing.
- The first market teaches you who you are: Korea, Reddit, YC, SF, enterprise inbound, and open-source users each reflected a different version of the company back at us.
- Don't build for people you do not respect enough: ICP is not just segmentation; it is whether you actually care about their daily pain.
- The company is the essay: Public writing, investor updates, product pivots, and internal retros are all records of beliefs changing under pressure.
